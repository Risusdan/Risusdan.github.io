Reading Notes - The Elements of Style

Posted on 2024-08-22 Edited on 2024-08-27 In ReadingNotes Word count in article: 820 Reading time ≈ 3 mins.

This book provides clear, concise rules and principles for effective writing in English.

About the book

  • Author: William Strunk Jr.
  • Publication Date: 2018/09/27
  • Number of Pages: 239

Chapter 1 - Introductory

This book aims to give in brief space the principle requirements of plain English style.

Chapter 2 - Elementary Rules of Usage

Rule 1. Form the possessive singular of nouns by adding ’s.

  • Exceptions are the possessive of ancent proper names in -es and -is, the possessive Jesus’ and such forms for sinscience’ sake, for righteousness’ sake.

Charles’s friend
John’s poems
the king’s malice

Rule 2. In a series of three or more terms with a single conjunction, use a comma after each term except the last.

  • In the names of business firms the last comma is omitted as Smith, Jones and Company.

I have visited the museums of Cairo, Alexandria, and Thebes..

Rule 3. Enclose parenthetic expressions between commas.

  • Always to be reguared as parenthetic and to be enclosed between commas (or, at the end of the sentence, between comma and period) are the following:
    • the year, when forming part of the date
    • the day of the month, when following the day of the week
    • the abbreviations etc. and jr. ^1
    • non-restrictive relative clauses, and similar clauses introduced by conjunctions indicating time or place. ^2

My siser you will be please to hear, is going to be married. should be My siser, you will be please to hear, is going to be married.
^1: Hank tries not to eat apples, pears, etc., even though he likes them.
^2: The students, which had at first been rather noisy, soon settled down to work.

Rule 4. Place a comma before a conjunction introducing a co-ordinate clause.

  • Two-part sentences of which the second member is introduced by as (in the sense of because), for, or, nor, and while (in the sense of and at the same time) likewise require a comma before the conjunction.
  • If the second member is introduced by an adverb, a semicolon, not a comma, is required.
  • The uses of so (equivalent to accordingly or to so that) are somewhat colloquial and should be avoided in writing. A simple correction is to omit the word so and begin the first clause with as or since.^3
  • When the subject is the same for both clauses and is expressed only once, a comma is required if the connective is but.^4

^3: I had never been in the place before; so I had difficulty in finding my way about. should be As I had never been in the place before, I had difficulty in finding my way about.
^4: I have heard her sing, but am still undecided about her voice.

Rule 5. Do not join independent clauses by a comma.

  • If two or more clauses, grammatically complete and not joined by a conjunction, are to form a single compound sentence, the proper mark of punctuation is a semicolon.^5
  • It is of course equally correct to write the above as two sentences each, replacing the semicolon by periods.^6
  • If a conjunction is inserted the proper mark is a comma.^7
  • Note that if the second clause is preceded by an adverb, such as accordingly, besides, then, therefore, thus, and not by a conjunction, the semicolon is still required.^8

^5: I have a dog; he is black and white.
^6: I have a dog. He is black and white.
^7: I have a dog, and he is black and white.
^8: The weather was cold; therefore, we decided to stay indoors.

Rule 6. Do not break sentences in two.

  • In other words, do not use periods for commas.

I met them on a liner several years ago. Coming home from London to New York. should not be I met them on a liner several years ago, coming home from London to New York.

Rule 7. A participial phrase at the beginning of a sentence must refer to the grammatical subject.

On arriving in New York, our friends led us to the hotel. should be When we arrived in New York, our friends led us to the hotel.
Young and inexperienced, the task seemed easy to me. should be Young and inexperienced, I thought the task easy.

Chapter 3 - Elementary Principles of Composition

Rule 1. Make the paragraph the unit of composition: one paragraph to each topic.

Rule 2. As a rule, begin each paragraph with a topic sentence; end it in conformity with the beginning.

Rule 3. Use the active voice.

Rule 4. Put statements in positive form.

Rule 5. Use definite, specific, concrete language.

Rule 6. Omit needless words.

Rule 7. Avoid a succession of loose sentences.

Rule 8. Express co-ordinate ideas in similar form.

Rule 9. Keep related words together.

Rule 10. In summaries, keep to one tense.

Rule 11. Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end.
